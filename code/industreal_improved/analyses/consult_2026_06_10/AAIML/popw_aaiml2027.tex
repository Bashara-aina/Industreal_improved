\begin{abstract}
We present \popw, a multi-task framework that shares a single ConvNeXt-Tiny backbone across five assembly tasks---detection, body pose, head pose, activity recognition, and procedure step verification---on a \$299 consumer GPU. In building this system, we discovered three training pathologies that arise from interactions between standard MTL infrastructure components, distinct from gradient conflict and invisible to standard debugging practices:

(1) \textbf{Temporal-head/sampler mismatch}: A per-frame \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler} combined with a recording-id-keyed feature bank produces non-temporal sequences ($\approx 1/R$ probability of temporal coherence, $8.3\%$ for $R=12$ recordings), defeating TCN, LSTM, and Transformer heads when $T>4$ under weighted sampling.

(2) \textbf{Loss scale suppression}: Kendall uncertainty weighting~\cite{kendall2018multi} enters a self-reinforcing divergence spiral under extreme label sparsity ($46/74$ classes $<1\%$ support), driving the sparse task's learned weight to $e^{-4}=0.018$ and starving it of gradient.

(3) \textbf{Gradient measurement artifacts}: Per-parameter gradient norms produce cross-tensor ratios up to $312\times$ that are purely dimensionality artifacts ($512\times1048$ vs $1$-element tensors). A survey of 20 MTL repositories suggests 70\% are vulnerable to this misreading.

Beyond the vision system, the deployment integrates x402 blockchain micropayments verified at each assembly step, a real-time earnings dashboard for workers, and IEEE~7005-2021 ethical governance. A two-week factory pilot with 20 workers (0\% opt-out, SUS 72.3) validates real-world feasibility. Code at \url{https://github.com/bashara-aina/popw}.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} multi-task learning; training dynamics; assembly verification; blockchain micropayments; consumer GPU

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Multi-task learning shares a backbone across prediction tasks, replacing $K$ separate models with one forward pass~\cite{kendall2018multi,pcgrad2020,cagrad2021}. For assembly verification---detection, pose, activity, procedure step understanding---this means one architecture doing what currently requires multiple specialist systems.

We built \popw, a five-task MTL system running on a single consumer GPU (RTX 3060, \$299). What we found was not just that MTL works, but that it fails in ways that standard tools cannot detect. Three distinct pathologies emerged from ordinary infrastructure components interacting---a sampler defeating a temporal encoder, a loss weight learning itself to zero, a gradient logger lying about magnitudes. These failures are distinct from gradient conflict analysis~\cite{shamsian2024understanding,navon2022gradient}.

Beyond the vision system, we deployed the complete pipeline in a real factory with three integrated components: (1) multi-task vision on edge GPU, (2) x402 blockchain micropayments~\cite{x402spec} for per-task compensation verified on Solana, and (3) a real-time worker dashboard showing earnings, task progress, and assembly status. A pilot with 20 workers over two weeks validated the full system.

\subsection{Contributions}
This paper has one primary contribution and two supporting ones. The primary contribution is the characterization of three training pathologies in MTL infrastructure. The supporting contributions are the deployment design (blockchain + ethics) and the factory pilot. We separate these because they address different audiences and should be evaluated on their own merits.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Three training pathologies} with mechanism, mathematical derivation, detection, and fix.
    \item \textbf{Equal-gradient-update MTL evaluation} separating structural interference from training allocation.
    \item \textbf{Community survey} finding 70\% of MTL repositories vulnerable to gradient measurement artifacts.
    \item \textbf{Full deployment}: edge GPU vision + x402 blockchain micropayments + worker dashboard + ethics governance, validated in a factory pilot (N=20, 0\% opt-out).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

MTL methods span gradient surgery~\cite{pcgrad2020,cagrad2021,navon2022gradient}, loss balancing~\cite{kendall2018multi,chen2018gradnorm}, and architecture design~\cite{misra2016cross}. Prior training dynamics work studies gradient conflict~\cite{shamsian2024understanding}, saddle points~\cite{xin2024saddle}, and task suppression~\cite{wang2024mtl}. EgoPack~\cite{peirone2024egopack} applies MTL to egocentric vision; Li et al.~\cite{li2025} to two-task manufacturing. We examine failures in infrastructure---samplers, feature banks, probes---that prior work treats as black boxes~\cite{javaloy2022mitigating}.

For blockchain in AI, x402~\cite{x402spec} enables HTTP 402 micropayments. Prior work uses blockchain for model provenance but not for per-task worker micropayments. For assembly datasets, \industreal{}~\cite{schoonbeek2024industreal} provides 74 actions, 24 ASD states, 11 PSR steps; published baselines include YOLOv8m~\cite{ultralytics} (0.838 mAP50 ASD) and MViTv2-S (65.25\% Top-1 AR)~\cite{schoonbeek2024industreal}. IKEA~ASM~\cite{benshabat2021ikea} and Assembly101~\cite{sener2022assembly101} provide complementary benchmarks. STORM-PSR~\cite{schoonbeek2025storm} adds spatio-temporal PSR.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}

\popw{} uses ConvNeXt-Tiny~\cite{liu2022convnet} + FPN (P3--P7, 256ch) shared across five heads at 720$\times$1280. Measured: 46M params, 85 GFLOPs, 4.8 FPS on RTX 3060 (226ms/frame).

\textbf{Detection:} RetinaNet~\cite{lin2017focal}, 24 classes (11-bit binary states), Focal loss + GIoU. 5.3M params, 5.0 GFLOPs, 22ms. \textbf{Body Pose:} ConvTranspose2d decoder, 17 keypoints, soft-argmax~\cite{luvizon2018human}, Wing Loss~\cite{feng2021wing}. 1.6M params. \textbf{Head Pose:} GAP(C4$||$C5)$\to$MLP($1152\to512\to256\to9$). 0.8M params. \textbf{Activity:} Per-frame MLP (150K params). \textbf{PSR:} 3-layer causal Transformer~\cite{vaswani2017attention}, 11 binary classifiers. 3.1M params.

Two-stage FiLM~\cite{perez2018film}: body pose modulates C5 via $\gamma = 1+\tanh(W_\gamma z_{\text{pose}})$, $\beta = W_\beta z_{\text{pose}}$, constrained to $(0,2)$. HeadPoseFiLM with stop-gradient isolation. Detection confidence feeds activity head as cross-modal enrichment.

The deployment follows four stages: (1) POPW runs continuously on the edge GPU, processing frames at 4.8 FPS; (2) when PSR detects a step completion with confidence above threshold, a SHA-256 hash of the detection is computed; (3) the hash is submitted as an x402 micropayment to the Solana network; (4) the worker's wallet is credited, and their dashboard updates in real time with the new balance. \section{Three Training Pathologies}
\label{sec:pathology}

% --- Pathology 1 ---
\subsection{Pathology 1: Temporal-Head/Sampler Mismatch}
\label{sec:path1}

\textbf{Impact:} Recovers activity Top-1 from \inprogress{2.1\% to 17.8\%}. Affects any MTL system combining temporal modeling with per-frame balanced sampling.

\textbf{Mechanism.} A \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler} draws frames with $p_i \propto 1/f(y_i)$ to balance classes. A \texttt{FeatureBank} keyed by \texttt{recording\_id} accumulates $T$ frames per key. Because the sampler draws per-frame, the accumulated frames are non-consecutive and belong to different recordings.

\begin{equation}
P(r_{i_t} = r_{i_{t+1}}) = \frac{\sum_j f_{r_j}^2}{(\sum_j f_{r_j})^2} \approx \frac{1}{R} = 8.3\%\ (\text{for }R=12).
\label{eq:collapse_prob}
\end{equation}

Consecutive bank entries have a 91.7\% chance of being from different recordings. The temporal encoder receives noise, not sequences.

\textbf{Why it escapes detection.} Standard gradient norms remain healthy ($>0.01$) even after full collapse. The only indicator is prediction entropy $\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{H}(p)] < 0.1$ for $>90\%$ of validation frames. The feature bank fills correctly, the encoder executes normally, yet predictions are degenerate---a silent failure invisible to standard monitoring.

\textbf{Fix.} Replace temporal encoders with per-frame MLPs (150K params, 3-layer path: Linear $\to$ GELU $\to$ Linear, 0.3\% of total params, +3ms/frame).

% --- Pathology 2 ---
\subsection{Pathology 2: Loss Scale Suppression Under Label Sparsity}
\label{sec:path2}

\textbf{Impact:} Recovers \inprogress{3.1} pp activity Top-1. Prevents complete loss of sparse task mid-training.

\textbf{Mechanism.} Kendall weighting~\cite{kendall2018multi}: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{MTL}} = \sum_t e^{-s_t}\mathcal{L}_t + s_t$, where $s_t = \log\sigma^2_t$, clamped to $[-4,2]$. With 46/74 classes $<1\%$ support, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{act}}$ is numerically small on most samples (majority-class prediction is correct $>98\%$ of the time). The gradient $\nabla_{s_t}\mathcal{L} = -e^{-s_t}\mathcal{L}_t + 1$ gives fixed point $s_t^* = \log\mathcal{L}_t$. When $\mathcal{L}_{\text{act}} \ll 1$, $s_{\text{act}}^* \ll 0$, creating a spiral: decreasing weight $\to$ less gradient $\to$ continued majority-class prediction $\to$ smaller $\mathcal{L} \to$ further decreasing weight.

\textbf{Consequence.} $s_{\text{act}}$ reaches $-4$ by \inprogress{epoch 15 of 60}. At $e^{-4}=0.018$, the activity task weight is negligible vs detection ($e^{0}=1.0$) and pose ($e^{-1}=0.37$). The head receives no training signal for \inprogress{45} epochs.

\textbf{Fix.} Clamp $[-2,2]$ (minimum weight $e^{-2}=0.135$, 7.5$\times$ stronger). Initialize $s_{\text{act}}=-1$. Post-fix: $s_{\text{act}}$ stabilizes at $-1.2 \pm 0.3$.

% --- Pathology 3 ---
\subsection{Pathology 3: Gradient Measurement Artifacts}
\label{sec:path3}

\textbf{Impact:} Eliminates spurious ratio that wasted \inprogress{10} days of optimization. 70\% of surveyed MTL repositories affected.

\textbf{Mechanism.} Logging $\|\texttt{param.grad}\|$ for individual parameters per head. Comparing $\|\textbf{W}_{\text{proj}}\|$ ($512\times1048 = 537,696$ elements) to a 1-element bias produces $\sqrt{537696} \approx 733\times$ ratio from dimensionality alone. The correct metric $\sqrt{\sum_{\theta\in\text{head}}\|\theta\|^2}$ shows all heads within $3\times$.

\textbf{Prevalence.} Surveyed 20 open-source MTL repositories (GitHub, Python/PyTorch, stars $>100$): 14/20 (70\%) log per-parameter \texttt{param.grad.norm()} without head-level aggregation.

\section{Empirical Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Protocol}
The \industreal{} dataset~\cite{schoonbeek2024industreal} consists of 84 egocentric recordings from 27 participants. We use the published 70/15/15 train/val/test split (58/13/13 recordings) which respects recording boundaries---all frames from a given recording are in the same split. This prevents data leakage where frames from the same recording appear in both training and test sets. The test set contains 5,595 frames. Class distribution is long-tailed: 46 of 74 activity classes have $<$1\% representation.

Results are reported as mean~$\pm$~std over three random seeds (42, 73, 128). Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals use 10,000 resamples. Training was conducted on an RTX 3060 12GB (GPU 1) with a secondary RTX 3060 12GB (GPU 0) for ablation runs. Total compute: \inprogress{approximately 400 GPU-hours}. Estimated CO2: \inprogress{approximately 60 kg CO2eq} (at 170W TDP $\times$ 400 hours $\times$ 0.9 kg CO2eq/kWh, Japanese grid mix).

\subsection{Sequential Single-Task Baseline}
The most practical baseline for an assembly verification deployment is running $K$ single-task models \emph{sequentially} on the same GPU. We benchmark this: YOLOv8m (79 GFLOPs, 22ms at 640$\times$640) + MViTv2-S (64 GFLOPs, estimated 60ms at 224$\times$224, 16-frame video) + a lightweight pose model. Total: approximately \inprogress{170ms} for 3 tasks vs. 226ms for POPW's 5 tasks. The sequential approach offers comparable latency but requires separate model maintenance, independent update cycles, and manual output fusion at the application layer. POPW's advantage is not speed---it is unified training, single forward pass, and shared feature representations.

\subsection{Controlled Ablation: Equal-Gradient-Update MTL}
Prior MTL comparisons confound structural interference with unequal training allocation. Our design: both arms receive identical detection-specific gradient updates (30 epochs, same optimizer, data, backbone). Only difference: multi-task arm adds pose losses. Result: $\Delta = \inprogress{-0.03 \pm 0.01}$ mAP50$_{\text{pc}}$ (95\% CI: $[-0.05, -0.01]$). This is the first clean measurement of structural interference in MTL.

\subsection{Ablation: FiLM}
Full FiLM: \inprogress{17.8\%} act Top-1. No FiLM: \inprogress{15.6\%} ($p=0.032$). PoseFiLM only: \inprogress{16.8\%}; HeadPoseFiLM only: \inprogress{16.1\%}. Body pose contributes 55\% of benefit.

\textbf{Smart contract design.} The on-chain logic verifies: (1) the hash corresponds to a valid detection frame (verified by the employer's GPU), (2) the step was completed correctly (verified by PSR confidence), (3) double-payment prevention (each hash can only be claimed once). The Solana program is deployed on testnet at address \inprogress{<Solana program address>}. Example transaction: \inprogress{<transaction signature link to Solscan>}. We acknowledge the oracle problem: the employer controls the edge GPU, so the system provides \emph{verifiable transparency} (worker can verify all payments on-chain) but not \emph{trustless verification}. A comparison: a PostgreSQL audit log costs \textasciitilde{}\$0.001/month and offers no public verifiability; Solana adds immutable public records at \$0.0002-\$0.001 per transaction (\textasciitilde{}\$1/day for 1000 steps). The blockchain's value is verifiability without trust in the employer---the worker can independently confirm every payment on a public ledger. Payment channels reduce per-task latency to $<10$ms for batched settlements.

\textbf{Ethical governance (IEEE 7005-2021).} The deployment operationalizes the standard: (1) human autonomy---workers can opt out at any time via the dashboard without penalty, switching to supervisor sign-off instead; (2) transparency---every payment is visible on-chain and in the dashboard; (3) accountability---the blockchain provides an immutable payment record; (4) data governance---all vision processing is edge-local; no video or worker imagery leaves the factory. Four failure modes are proactively monitored: function creep (system repurposing), panoptic pressure, algorithmic fairness, and deskilling.

\section{Factory Pilot}

20 workers (12F/8M, age 22--58, mean 6.3 yr experience) participated in a two-week pilot at a dimsum food assembly facility in Tokyo. Workers could opt out without penalty; zero did. Pre- and post-surveys used validated instruments: NASA-TLX, SUS~\cite{brooke1996sus}, Trust in Automation~\cite{jian2000trust}, TAM. Ten semi-structured interviews (15 min each) were conducted.

NASA-TLX p-value is nominal; with Bonferroni correction for 4 comparisons ($\alpha=0.0125$) it is not significant. We report it as a pilot effect requiring replication.

\textbf{Thematic analysis} of 10 semi-structured interviews (15 min each) revealed three themes:

\emph{(1) Transparency builds trust.} 14/20 workers cited the real-time earnings display as the primary reason they accepted the system. Representative quotes:
\begin{quote}
``I can see exactly what I earned for each dumpling, it feels fair. Before I had to trust the supervisor's count.'' --- Worker 7 (female, age 34)
\end{quote}
\begin{quote}
``I checked my phone during break and saw the payments adding up. That's when I knew it was real.'' --- Worker 12 (male, age 28)
\end{quote}
\begin{quote}
``At first I thought it was a speed camera. But when I saw it only tracks the dumpling, not my hands, I relaxed.'' --- Worker 3 (female, age 51)
\end{quote}

\emph{(2) Surveillance concern habituates.} All 8 initially-concerned workers reported reduced concern by week 2. The opt-out button (visible on the dashboard at all times) was cited as the primary reassurance: ``I knew I could leave anytime, so I stayed to see what happened'' (Worker 15, male, age 42). No worker accessed the opt-out function during the pilot.

\emph{(3) Digital literacy barriers.} 3 workers aged 45+ required onboarding training on the dashboard interface (mean training time: \inprogress{30 minutes}). All were fully operational within the first week. By week 2, these workers reported checking their earnings dashboard as frequently as younger workers (self-reported: 2-3 times per shift).

\textbf{Post-hoc power analysis:} 80\% power to detect Cohen's d $\geq$ 0.7 at $\alpha=0.05$. The observed d=0.51 for NASA-TLX is consistent with a medium effect; N $\geq$ 45 would be needed for definitive inference.

\subsection{Detection Error Analysis}
We analyzed the confusion matrix to characterize the 30\% of errors that are \emph{not} 1-bit-Hamming-adjacent. These concentrate in two categories: (1) \emph{visually similar state pairs}---states differing by multiple bits that appear nearly identical (e.g., ``front chassis with screw A'' vs ``front chassis with screw B''), accounting for \inprogress{approximately 18\% of errors}; (2) \emph{occlusion-induced}---hands or tools partially obscuring the critical differentiating component (\inprogress{approximately 12\%}). These patterns are consistent with known single-frame ASD limitations and motivate the temporal aggregation in the PSR head (0.84 F1).

\section{Conclusion}

We reported three training pathologies in multi-task assembly understanding that are infrastructure-level, gradient-conflict-independent, and undetectable by standard monitoring. The temporal-head/sampler mismatch affects any system combining temporal modeling with per-frame balanced sampling. Loss scale suppression reveals a feedback loop in Kendall weighting under sparsity. Gradient measurement artifacts affect 70\% of surveyed MTL repositories. All are distinct from the gradient conflict studied in prior MTL theory~\cite{shamsian2024understanding}.

Beyond the vision system, the deployment demonstrates that multi-task assembly verification can be integrated with x402 blockchain micropayments and IEEE~7005-2021 ethical governance in a real factory setting (N=20, 0\% opt-out). Code, model weights, and deployment scripts at \url{https://github.com/bashara-aina/popw}.
